The Bitter Lesson for Sequence Models

May 2026

You are designing a sequence mixer. You have three things to optimize: expressivity (what functions the layer can represent), hardware efficiency (how many FLOPs you get per wall-clock second), and training stability (how reliably the layer learns at scale). You cannot maximize all three. Which do you sacrifice?

I want to put forward a hypothesis. I am not certain it is correct. I personally hope it is not. But the evidence I have seen makes me think the answer might be expressivity. Expressivity is the theoretically beautiful axis. It connects to formal language theory, to circuit complexity, to the fundamental question of what computation a sequence model can perform. And yet the empirical record keeps suggesting that models which trade expressivity for efficiency and stability tend to win.

If this hypothesis holds, it is a version of Sutton's bitter lesson applied to the internal design of sequence models. General methods that scale with compute beat clever methods that encode structure. The structure helps on paper. The compute helps on benchmarks. I want to lay out why I think this story might be true, where the evidence is strong, and where I am genuinely uncertain.

The trifecta

The three axes are not independent, but they pull in different directions often enough that they form a real design constraint.

Expressivity is the set of input-output mappings a single layer can realize. A layer with a large, learnable state that updates nonlinearly per token can, in principle, track richer functions of the input history. TTT-MLP is an extreme example: the mixer is literally gradient descent on a per-sequence model, giving it Turing-complete per-layer expressivity in the limit.

Hardware efficiency is how well the layer maps onto actual GPUs. This means large matrix multiplies, minimal memory bandwidth pressure, and parallelism across the sequence dimension during training. A layer that requires sequential per-token state updates with nonlinear dependencies is expressive but slow. A layer that decomposes into chunked matrix products is fast but constrained.

Training stability is how reliably the layer learns when you stack many copies of it and train on billions of tokens. Complex recurrent updates develop exploding or vanishing gradients. Nonlinear state transitions create loss landscape pathologies. Simple, well-conditioned layers with forget gates and normalizations train cleanly. Complex layers require careful initialization, custom learning rate schedules, and sometimes just don't work at scale.

The expressivity temptation

The theoretical case for expressivity is real and well-developed. Merrill and Sabharwal formalize the notion of state tracking: can a model, reading a sequence of instructions from a finite group, track the composed state? Transformers with hard attention can. Soft-attention transformers struggle. Linear recurrences provably cannot, regardless of dimension. The hierarchy is crisp and mathematically satisfying.

More broadly, there is a line of work connecting sequence model expressivity to circuit complexity classes. Fixed-depth transformers sit in $\text{TC}^0$. Adding recurrence or depth pushes into higher classes. The implication is that certain sequence-to-sequence functions are simply inaccessible to shallow, parallelizable architectures.

These results motivate architectures like TTT-MLP, RWKV's complex-valued recurrences, and various nonlinear SSM variants that try to increase per-layer expressivity. The logic is straightforward: if linear recurrences provably cannot track state, give the recurrence a nonlinear update rule. If softmax attention cannot represent certain functions at fixed depth, make the attention mechanism more powerful.

The problem is not that these arguments are wrong. They are correct. The question is whether they optimize the axis that matters most in practice.

Bias-variance for architectures

The classical bias-variance decomposition says that prediction error has two components. Bias is the error from underfitting. A model class that cannot represent the target function will have irreducible bias. Variance is the error from overfitting to noise in the training process. A model class that is too flexible will fit different training runs differently.

Architecture design has an analogous tradeoff, though the terms map differently. A more expressive mixer has lower approximation bias. It can, in principle, represent harder functions per layer. But expressivity comes at a cost that shows up as something like variance. Not variance in the statistical sense, but variance in the engineering sense: harder to train stably, less hardware-efficient (so fewer layers for the same compute budget), and more sensitive to hyperparameters.

Consider a concrete comparison. You have a fixed FLOP budget. You can spend it on one layer of TTT-MLP, which runs gradient descent internally and can in principle represent arbitrary sequence functions. Or you can spend the same FLOPs on four layers of gated DeltaNet , each of which is a simple linear recurrence with a forget gate and a delta-rule update. Each DeltaNet layer is individually less expressive. But four of them, interleaved with MLPs, compose into a deep network that is empirically competitive or better.

Why? The same reason bagging beats a single complex model. Each simple layer is well-conditioned. Gradients flow cleanly through the forget gates. The chunkwise-parallel decomposition maps perfectly onto GPU matrix units. You can train it stably at scale with standard optimizers. The deep composition of simple layers covers the function space that matters in practice, even though each individual layer is theoretically limited.

The expressivity gap is real in the worst case. A single DeltaNet layer cannot track a finite group action. But the relevant question is not what a single layer can do in the worst case. It is what a deep stack of simple layers can do on the distribution of actual language, trained with actual gradient descent, at actual scale.

Chain of thought breaks the ceiling

The strongest version of the expressivity argument is about inherent computational limits. Fixed-depth transformers are in $\text{TC}^0$. They cannot solve problems that require serial computation, like composing $n$ permutations or evaluating Boolean formulas of unbounded depth. No amount of width compensates for insufficient depth. This is a real barrier.

But chain-of-thought reasoning sidesteps it entirely. Feng, Guo, et al. show that autoregressive transformers with chain-of-thought can solve problems requiring serial computation. The mechanism is simple: each generated token is an intermediate computation step, effectively adding depth at inference time. The model trades tokens for depth. Serial problems that are impossible at fixed depth become tractable when the model can write and read intermediate results.

This changes the calculus of the trifecta. If you need serial computation, you have two options. You can build a per-layer mechanism that is inherently sequential and expressive (TTT, complex-valued recurrences, nonlinear SSMs). Or you can use a cheap, parallel, stable mixer and let chain-of-thought provide the serial computation at inference time.

If this decomposition works in practice, the second option is better on two of the three axes. The mixer is hardware-efficient and training-stable. The serial computation happens in token space, which is already optimized for autoregressive generation. You pay in inference tokens, not in architectural complexity. Whether this tradeoff is always favorable is an open question.

This does not mean expressivity is irrelevant at the layer level. A mixer that is too weak will require too many CoT tokens, making inference expensive. There is a Pareto frontier between layer expressivity and CoT length. But the frontier is asymmetric. Marginal improvements in layer expressivity buy small reductions in CoT length at large costs in efficiency and stability. Marginal improvements in efficiency buy more layers, more data, more scale, and these compound.

The frontier is not symmetric

Put the three axes on a plot. On one side, architectures that maximize per-layer expressivity: TTT-MLP, complex recurrences, nonlinear SSMs. On the other, architectures that maximize hardware efficiency and training stability: gated DeltaNet, Mamba2, GLA, simple gated linear recurrences with well-chosen parameterizations.

As far as I can tell, the empirical Pareto frontier is not centered. It appears biased toward the efficiency-stability side. At the compute budgets where we have good comparisons, the simple-but-stackable architectures tend to match or beat the complex-but-expressive ones. If this pattern is real and not an artifact of where we have looked, the bias-variance framing offers an explanation: variance penalties compound with depth and data, while bias penalties shrink as composition covers more of the function space.

I want to be clear about two things. First, this is a hypothesis, not a theorem. The evidence is suggestive but not conclusive. We do not have controlled experiments across enough scales and tasks to rule out confounds. Maybe the expressive architectures have not been tuned as carefully. Maybe the benchmarks we use do not stress the right capabilities. Maybe the gap reverses at a scale no one has reached yet.

Second, I find this somewhat unfortunate. The expressivity results are beautiful mathematics. The state tracking hierarchy is elegant. The circuit complexity connections are deep. And they are telling us something true about these architectures. A single gated linear recurrence really cannot track a finite group action. A fixed-depth transformer really is in $\text{TC}^0$. These are facts, and they matter.

The question is whether "true" and "useful for predicting which architecture wins at scale" are the same claim. My read of the current evidence is that the theoretical gaps are real but do not dominate the Pareto frontier, because the other two axes compound with scale in ways that expressivity does not. But I hold this view with moderate confidence. There is a plausible world where expressivity bottlenecks emerge at frontier scale, or where someone finds a layer that is simultaneously expressive, efficient, and stable, and this entire argument becomes a historical footnote.

Sutton's original bitter lesson is about search and learning versus human knowledge. The version here is narrower but structurally similar. Human knowledge about what computation a sequence mixer should perform (state tracking, group actions, serial composition) may be less important than giving the system more compute to figure it out. And "giving the system more compute" means, concretely, making each layer cheap enough to stack deeply, stable enough to train at scale, and fast enough to process data quickly. The expressivity follows from depth, data, and chain-of-thought. It may not need to be designed in.

I want this to be wrong. The alternative, that the field converges on a few simple, well-engineered primitives and the beautiful theory becomes a footnote, is the less interesting outcome. But I think we should be cognizant of the story the evidence is currently telling, even if we hope the next chapter reads differently.
