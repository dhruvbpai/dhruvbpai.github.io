The Loss Curve Ouroboros

May 2025

What happens if you take a model's loss curve, rescale it into a learning rate schedule, and use it to train the next run? And then repeat forever?

Click and drag to rotate the Ouroboros.

Sometimes it's fun to ask ridiculous quesitions. As an example, what if we take the loss curve from a training run, rescale it into a valid learning rate schedule, and use it as the schedule for the next run? And then we repeat this process ad infinitum, forming a fixed function map. The loss curve "eats" its own tail (hence Ouroborous) and each generation's output becomes the next generation's input through LR scheduling. We get this bizarre dynamical system over the space of LR schedules, and I spent last night looking at the eovlution of this system.

Let's walk through an example. We start with a standard warmup-stable-decay (WSD) schedule. The model trains well and loss drops like a power law. When training is done, we rescale that loss curve into an LR range for the next run. Since the loss decayed to near zero, the resulting schedule starts high but quickly falls to almost nothing. Training with that schedule barely moves the model, since the model spends most of learning at very low LR. As a result, the loss stays high for this iteration. However, high, flat loss curve becomes a high, flat LR schedule, which allows the next generation to actually converge again. And this cycle may repeat infinitely, or diverge! It's a really interesting problem, and it's nontrivial whether the system converges to a fixed point, a limit cycle, or full on chaos.

Formalizing the problem

Surprisingly, we can benefit nontrivially from formalizing this absurd problem. Suppose we have a model with parameters $\theta$ trained by vanilla gradient descent on a loss $\mathcal{L}$. Given a learning rate schedule $\eta : [0, T] \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ which maps from timestep to LR, the training dynamics produce a unique loss curve $L : [0, T] \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ defined by

$$L(t) = \mathcal{L}(\theta(t))$$

where $\theta(t)$ evolves according to

$$\dot{\theta}(t) = -\eta(t) \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta(t))$$

The loss curve depends on the schedule, so we can think of it as a functional $\mathcal{F}$ that maps schedules to loss curves.

$$\mathcal{F} : \eta(\cdot) \mapsto L(\cdot)$$

For the other half ot the loop, the rescaling operator $\mathcal{R}$ takes a loss curve and produces a learning rate schedule by normalizing it into a target range $[\eta_{\min}, \eta_{\max}]$.

$$\mathcal{R}[L](t) = \eta_{\min} + (\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min}) \cdot \frac{L(t) - L_{\min}}{L_{\max} - L_{\min}}$$

where $L_{\min} = \min_t L(t)$ and $L_{\max} = \max_t L(t)$. Intuitively, high loss maps to high learning rate and low loss maps to low learning rate.

The ouroboros is the composition $\mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}$. Starting from some seed schedule $\eta^{(0)}$, we iterate

$$\eta^{(n+1)} = \mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}[\eta^{(n)}]$$

A fixed point of this iteration is a schedule $\eta^*$ satisfying $\eta^* = \mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}[\eta^*]$. In other words, a schedule whose loss curve, when rescaled, reproduces itself exactly!

Critically, the schedule enters the dynamics only through the integrated schedule $A(t) = \int_0^t \eta(\tau) \, d\tau$. This means $\dot{A}(t) = \eta(t)$, so at a fixed point we get an ODE

$$\dot{A}(t) = \mathcal{R}[\mathcal{F}_A(A(t))]$$

where $\mathcal{F}_A$ writes the loss as a function of the cumulative schedule $A$ rather than time directly. This is a first-order autonomous ODE in $A$, which is a tractable object to study as opposed to our original functional fixed-point equation.

Example 1: Linear autoencoder

In the linear autoencoder case, we will show $\mathcal{F}$ has a closed form. Consider data $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$ with covariance $\Sigma = U \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_d) U^\top$ where $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_d > 0$. Our model is a rank-k linear autoencoder $\hat{x} = W^\top W x$ with $W \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$. The MSE loss is

$$\mathcal{L}(W) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}\left[\|x - W^\top W x\|^2\right]$$

This is the setting analyzed by Baldi & Hornik (1989) and Saxe et al. (2014). The key insight is that the dynamics decouple in the eigenbasis of $\Sigma$, the dynamics decouple. Each eigenmode $i$ has a projection strength $p_i(t) \in [0, 1]$ measuring how much of eigendirection $i$ the network has captured. The loss decomposes into the sum of the losses for each eigenmode.

$$L(t) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i \left(1 - p_i(t)\right)^2$$

Under gradient flow with schedule $\eta(t)$, each mode evolves independently according to a logistic equation

$$\dot{p}_i = 2\eta(t) \, \lambda_i \, p_i(1 - p_i)$$

Since the right-hand side factors as $\eta(t)$ times a function of $p_i$ alone, the schedule enters only through the integrated schedule $A(t) = \int_0^t \eta(\tau) \, d\tau$ as desired.

$$p_i(t) = \sigma\!\left(2\lambda_i A(t) + \operatorname{logit}(p_i(0))\right)$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $\operatorname{logit}(p) = \log(p/(1-p))$. Intuitively, each mode follows a sigmoid in the cumulative schedule $A$, with rate proportional to its eigenvalue $\lambda_i$. Big eigenvalues get learned first while small ones take exponentially longer.

Substituting back, we get the closed-form training operator

$$\mathcal{F}[\eta](t) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i \left(1 - \sigma\!\left(2\lambda_i A(t) + c_i\right)\right)^2$$

where $c_i = \operatorname{logit}(p_i(0))$ and $A(t) = \int_0^t \eta(\tau) \, d\tau$. $\mathcal{F}$ is explicitly a function of the schedule, and is a sum of squared sigmoid tails each indexed by an eigenvalue.

The ouroboros iteration $\eta^{(n+1)} = \mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}[\eta^{(n)}]$ is now fully computabl and the fixed-point ODE becomes

$$\dot{A} = \eta_{\min} + (\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min}) \cdot \frac{g(A) - g_{\min}}{g_{\max} - g_{\min}}$$

where $g(A) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i \lambda_i (1 - \sigma(2\lambda_i A + c_i))^2$ is the loss as a function of the cumulative schedule. Since this is a separable first-order ODE and the right-hand side is a known function of $A$ alone, we can solve it (at least numerically) and study its fixed points directly!

To see why the linear case converged to a fixed point, observe that $g(A)$ is monotonically decreasing. Since more learning generally means lower loss, $\mathcal{R}[g(A)]$ maps high-$A$ regions to low LR and low-$A$ regions to high LR -> which is a contraction. Intuitively, if the current schedule overshoots (converges too fast), the resulting loss curve is low, producing a low-LR schedule next round that undershoots. The overshoot shrinks each generation because the map is smooth and the composition $\mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}$ has bounded derivative less than 1 in the relevant regime, and so the oscillations damp out and the iteration converges.

Example 2: Nonlinear autoencoder

The linear case is clean but a bit boring. Every configuration converges to a fixed point, so the map does not have interesting behavior. When we add nonlinearity, the story changes entirely. We replace the linear autoencoder with a ReLU network ($6 \to 12 \to 2 \to 12 \to 6$) and use full-batch gradient descent on the same fixed Gaussian dataset. The resulting system no longer has a closed form for $\mathcal{F}$, and so we must study it numerically.

The loss landscape has non-smooth regions from ReLU, so high learning rates can cause loss spikes, which feed back as high-LR regions in the next schedule and potentially cause more spikes. Since Tte map $\mathcal{R} \circ \mathcal{F}$ can have derivative greater than 1, the iteration can oscillate permanently or behave chaotically.

We run numerical simulations to study the behavior of the system, and granularly sweep two hyperparameters. The rescaling operator gets a power-law exponent $\gamma$ that controls how aggressively loss maps to LR ($\gamma = 1$ is linear, $\gamma > 1$ amplifies high-loss regions), We sweep $\gamma$ against $\eta_{\max}$ and classify each pixel by its long-run behavior.

Phase diagram of the nonlinear ouroboros.

The boundary between fixed points and limit cycles may remind one of fractal behavior one may find in the mandelbrot set or other iterated maps. The ouroboros is a function iteration on the infinite-dimensional space of LR schedules, and the transition to chaos follows the same period-doubling route that Feigenbaum described for scalar maps. The only difference is that our "map" involves training a neural network, and so the nonlinearity comes from the loss landscape and training dynamics rather than from a simple polynomial.

As far as an exercise in free will goes, I found the dynamics produced by the ouroboros to be beautiful. It's also nice that thelinear case has a clsoed form solution with clean contraction. The nonlinear case gives us period doubling, chaos, and fractal phase boundaries.
